U+05B0 HEBREW POINT SHEVA

See in UniView

Hebrew ͓ שְׁוָא ʃvɑ e̞ ∅

e /∅ Expresses either a very short vowel, or is not pronounced, depending on the context, eg. it is pronounced in נְמָלִים, but not pronounced in גְּמָלִים. See a list of various contexts in which the sh'va is pronounced.

The sh'va is also used in conjunction with one of 3 other niqqud. In these cases, precomposed code points should be used. See ֱ   [U+05B1 HEBREW POINT HATAF SEGOL], ֲ   [U+05B2 HEBREW POINT HATAF PATAH], and ֳ   [U+05B3 HEBREW POINT HATAF QAMATS].

U+05B1 HEBREW POINT HATAF SEGOL

See in UniView

Hebrew ĕ חֲטַף סֶגּוֹל‬ ħaˈtaf sɛˈɡol e̞

e Formerly, this represented a very short vowel sound, but length is not phonetically distinguished in modern Israeli Hebrew. The orthographic distinction is, however, still maintained.w

Represents a combination of sh'va (see ְ   [U+05B0 HEBREW POINT SHEVA] with sɛˈɡol (see ֶ   [U+05B6 HEBREW POINT SEGOL]). This precomposed character should be used for the combination, rather than 2 separate code points. It is not decomposed during normalisation.

U+05B8 HEBREW POINT QAMATS

See in UniView

Description in the Unicode standard:

• used generically or as qamats gadol in orthography which distinguishes that from qamats qatan
→ (hebrew point qamats qatan - 05C7)

Hebrew ɑ קָמַץ kaˈmats ä o̞

a/o eg. שָׁנָה. Formerly, this represented both a short o vowel sound and a long a vowel sound, but length is not phonetically distinguished in modern Israeli Hebrew. The orthographic distinction is, however, still maintained.w To reduce ambiguity, though, modern Isreali writing tends to use וֹ [U+05D5 HEBREW LETTER VAV + U+05B9 HEBREW POINT HOLAM] instead for the o sound.w

See also ֳ   [U+05B3 HEBREW POINT HATAF QAMATS], which formerly represented a very short o sound.

In some biblical texts a distinction is made between ׇ [U+05C7 HEBREW POINT QAMATS QATAN] and קָמַץ גָּדוֹל kamaʦ gadɔl. Normally, in modern Hebrew, you can use this character for either, but where a distinction in needed you can use this character for kamaʦ gadɔl and ׇ [U+05C7 HEBREW POINT QAMATS QATAN] for kamaʦ katan.

U+05B9 HEBREW POINT HOLAM

See in UniView

Hebrew o חוֹלָם‬ ħoˈlam o̞

o Can be used alone with a consonant that is not a mater lectionis, eg. פֹּה.

However, it is typically used in the combination וֹ [U+05D5 HEBREW LETTER VAV + U+05B9 HEBREW POINT HOLAM], eg. קוֹל.

When this point is followed by א [U+05D0 HEBREW LETTER ALEF] the dot may appear over the top-right corner of the alef (although font support for that varies), eg. לֹא. If, however, the alef is used as a consonant (ie. it can take its own points), the dot appears at the top-left of the base character it follows, eg. תֹּאַר.w

More details are available at Wikipedia.

U+05BC HEBREW POINT DAGESH OR MAPIQ

See in UniView

Description in the Unicode standard:

= shuruq
• falls within the base letter

Hebrew ̣ דָּגֵשׁ dagesh/mappiq

Consonant hardener. The only diacritic to appear inside a consonant, this character is used in vowelled text with 5 characters to map to 'hard' sounds. This is similar to the distinction made in Syriac.

p פּ [U+05E4 HEBREW LETTER PE + U+05BC HEBREW POINT DAGESH OR MAPIQ]  (without dagesh, f).

b בּ [U+05D1 HEBREW LETTER BET + U+05BC HEBREW POINT DAGESH OR MAPIQ]   (without dagesh, v).

t תּ [U+05EA HEBREW LETTER TAV + U+05BC HEBREW POINT DAGESH OR MAPIQ]   (without dagesh also t, because the distinction was lost over time).

k כּ [U+05DB HEBREW LETTER KAF + U+05BC HEBREW POINT DAGESH OR MAPIQ]   (without dagesh, x/χ).

-k ךּ [U+05DA HEBREW LETTER FINAL KAF + U+05BC HEBREW POINT DAGESH OR MAPIQ] 

Vowel sign. In vowelled text, this character is used in the combination וּ [U+05D5 HEBREW LETTER VAV + U+05BC HEBREW POINT DAGESH OR MAPIQ] to represent the vowel o, eg. מוּם.

General sources: Daniels, Unicode, Wikipedia

U+05BE HEBREW PUNCTUATION MAQAF

See in UniView

Hebrew - מַקָּף‬ maqaf

Used as a hyphen between words, eg. תל־אביב ṫl-ʔvyv Tel Aviv.

It is well used in typography, but less common online because it is not always available on keyboards. Therefore, - [U+002D HYPHEN-MINUS] is often substituted, even though the position of that character is too low when displayed.

U+05C1 HEBREW POINT SHIN DOT

See in UniView

Hebrew ̎

Hebrew has two phonemes, s and ʃ, that are represented by a single consonant character, ש [U+05E9 HEBREW LETTER SHIN]. If it is necessary to indicate which is intended, two diacritics used only with this character, do the job: ׂ [U+05C2 HEBREW POINT SIN DOT] and ׁ [U+05C1 HEBREW POINT SHIN DOT]. They look identical, but the side to which they are positioned makes the difference.

This character appears top right, ie. שׁ, and produces ʃ.

General sources: Daniels, Unicode, Wikipedia

U+05C6 HEBREW PUNCTUATION NUN HAFUKHA

See in UniView

Hebrew punctuation, reversed nun / inverted nun / nun hafukha / nun menuzerret

Rare biblical character that is not part of a word or read aloud in any way, but serves solely to mark up text. Used surrounded by spaces. wp

"While it depends on the particular manuscript or printed edition, it is found in nine places: twice in the Book of Numbers (prior to and after Numbers 10:34-36), and seven times in Psalm 107. It is uncertain today what it was intended to signify." wp

There are numerous different glyph forms for this character, depending on the manuscript. For examples, see wikipedia.

Refs: Wikipedia

U+05F3 HEBREW PUNCTUATION GERESH

See in UniView

Description in the Unicode standard:

→ (apostrophe - 0027)

Hebrew ´ גֵּרֶשׁ ˈɡeʁeʃ

Hebrew equivalent of a period in abbreviations.

Also used to modify consonants to create non-native sounds.

The following are found in slang and loanwords that are part of the everyday Hebrew colloquial vocabulary:

  • ʧ with צ or ץ, eg. ריצ׳רץ׳.
  • ʤ with ג , eg. ג׳וּק.
  • ʒ with ז , eg. זָ׳רְגוֹן.
  • w as non-standard orthography for transcriptions, eg. עָוָנְטָה. This sound can also be written using װ [U+05F0 HEBREW LIGATURE YIDDISH DOUBLE VAV].

Geresh is also used to transcribe other sounds not in the hebrew phonology when transliterating (rather than adoption via loanwords).

  • ð with ד, ie. Arabic ذ, eg. ד׳ו אל-חיג׳ה d´v̇ ʔl-xyg´h Dhu al-Hijjah (ذو الحجة). Also used for the English voiced th. Sometimes just ד is used.
  • θ with ת or ץ, ie. Arabic ﺙ, eg. ת׳רסטון ṫ´ʁstv̇n̽ Thurston.
  • χ with ח, ie. Arabic خ, eg. שייח׳ ṡyyx´ Sheikh (شيخ). Used to specify χ rather than the possible h of ח.
  • ʁ with ר, ie. Arabic غ, eg. ר׳ג׳ר ʁ´g´ʁ Ghajar.

Where keyboards do not support this character, ASCII U+0027 APOSTROPHE is commonly used. This is visually substandard, however, since it doesn't align with the top of the hebrew characters like geresh.

General sources: Daniels, Unicode, Wikipedia
