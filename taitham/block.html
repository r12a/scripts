This page lists characters in the following Unicode block and provides information about them.

  • Tai Tham

This is not authoritative, peer-reviewed information – these are just notes I have gathered and copied from various places.

For details about how the script works, see Tai Tham/Khün, and Northern Thai. For further information related to this and other scripts, see the Script links pages.

In order to allow the fonts to work on all platforms, rather than use '-' as a generic base for vowels and diacritics, this document uses the character WA, which is a simple circle. The transliterations are home-grown and language-specific.

We have usage data for the following 2 languages that use the Tai Tham script: Khün, Northern Thai.

Used as a final r (pronounced n).

Owen says that the superscript consonants are limited to final r (this character) and ŋ ( ◌ᩙ [U+1A59 TAI THAM CONSONANT SIGN FINAL NGA​]) in syllables where a subscript vowel prevents the use of a subscript final consonant. Northern Thai

Used in Northern Thai to elide the sound(s) it follows. The precise range of characters not to be sounded is not defined (that is, it’s a reading rule), although it does not extend beyond one cluster.e

It can be used:

  • to indicate a final consonant by removing the inherent vowel sign, such as ᩃ᩺ ḻ˟,
  • in the commonest use, over a stack of two consonants, to remove the implicit vowel. As Tai syllables can only have one final consonant, the second (and graphically lower) consonant is then automatically dropped.r
  • to indicate an elided consonant sound, eg. the l in ᨼᩥᩃ᩠ᨾ᩺ f̱iḻ˖m̱r̽ fim² film,c

U+1A7B TAI THAM SIGN MAI SAM

Northern Thai

Used at the end of a word to indicate that it should be repeated, eg. ᨲ᩵ᩣ᩠ᨦ t¹ā˖ŋ̱ taːŋ to be different, vs. ᨲ᩵ᩣ᩠ᨦ᩻ t¹ā˖ŋ̱ʻ taːŋ taːŋ different in my view. e

A third use is somewhat different. A subjoined letter may be medial, final, or the start of a new syllable. Mai sam can be used to indicate that a consonant begins a new syllable, in which case it is stored following the subjoined form and indicates that it is the consonant that starts the new syllable, eg. compare ᨳᩫ᩠ᨶᩁ tʰɔ̈˖ṉr tʰonra with ᨳᩫ᩠ᨶ᩻ᩁ tʰɔ̈˖ṉʻr tʰanon path (final r is pronounced as n). e
