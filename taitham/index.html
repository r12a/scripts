This page provides basic information about the Tai Tham script and its use for the Northern Thai (Lanna) and Khün languages. It is not authoritative, peer-reviewed information – these are just notes I have gathered or copied from various places as i learned. For character-specific details follow the links to the Tai Tham character notes.

For similar information related to other scripts, see the Script comparison table.

Usage & history

From Unicode:

The script called Tai Tham is used for three living languages, Lue, Khuen, and Northern Thai, which are spoken in China, Myanmar, Northern Thailand, and surrounding areas. In addition, the script is used for Lao Tham (or Old Lao) and other dialect variants found in Buddhist palm leaves and notebooks. Although the script has no single, commonly recognized name across the region today, it is known by various language-specific and region-specific names, such as Old Xishuang banna Dai or Old Tai Lue in China, Khün in Myanmar, and Tua Mueang, Lanna, or Yuan in Thailand.

Few of the six million speakers of Northern Thai are literate in the Tai Tham script, although there is some rising interest in the script among the young. There are about 690,000 speakers of Tai Lue. Of those, many people born before 1950 are literate in the Tai Tham script, and newspapers and other literature are regularly produced in the Xishuangbanna region of Yunnan using the script. Younger speakers are taught the New Tai Lue script, instead. ... The Tai Tham script continues to be taught in the Tai Lue monasteries. There are 107,000 speakers of Khün, for which Tai Tham is the only script.

 

From Wikipedia:

The Tai Tham script, Lanna script (Thai: อักษรธรรมล้านนา) or Tua Mueang (Lanna: ᨲ᩠ᩅᩫᨾᩮᩥᩬᨦ, Northern Thai pronunciation: [tǔa.mɯ̄aŋ] Tai Lü: ᨲᩫ᩠ᩅᨵᨾ᩠ᨾ᩼ , Tham, "scripture"), is used for three living languages: Northern Thai (that is, Kham Mueang), Tai Lü and Khün. In addition, the Lanna script is used for Lao Tham (or old Lao) and other dialect variants in Buddhist palm leaves and notebooks. The script is also known as Tham or Yuan script.

Key features

The Tai Tham script is an abugida, ie. consonants carry an inherent vowel sound that is overridden using vowel signs. In Tai Tham, consonants carry an inherent vowel a. See the table to the right for a brief overview of features, taken from the Script Comparison Table.

The script tries to create vertical stacks where possible, but only one character at a time can appear below the base onset. This means that syllable-final consonants tend to appear below the initial consonant, however if they follow a subscript vowel-sign they are rendered alongside the onset consonant. A particularly unusual feature of the Tham script is that the virama can be used to subjoin a consonant after a dependent vowel sign, eg. ᩈᩣ᩠ᨾ sā˖m̱ saːm¹ three. Orthographic syllable clusters are unusually complicated, and sometimes require input of characters in a different order from that in which they are spoken.

Tai Tham is also unusual among abugidas in that the virama doesn't kill the vowel between consonants – it really serves just to indicate stacking opportunities.

Vowel-signs can appear above, below, to the left or to the right of the base consonant, and sometimes all four together.

Text runs from left to right.

Basic consonants

The top line shows the character in a font styled for Northern Thai, the second for Khün. There are some significant and consistent differences. Where two phonetic transcriptions are given, the higher one is for Northern Thai, the lower for Khün. Where a character is not used by a language, '–' is used.

These consonants are associated with high, mid, or low tone values. Low tone consonants are indicated using an underline in the transliteration. High and low consonants usually come in pairs, but where they don't the high variant is given by subjoining the low consonant below ᩉ [U+1A49 TAI THAM LETTER HIGH HA], eg. ᩉ᩠ᨶ h˖ṉ.

The consonants that default to mid tone are ᨯ ᨷ ᩋ.

ᩋ [U+1A4B TAI THAM LETTER A] represents a glottal stop. It can be used with vowels at the beginning of a syllable, eg. ᩋᩧ᩠ᨷ ʔɯ˖b ɯp² starve. Note that the Northern Thai shape (ᩋ) and the Khün shape (ᩋ) are very different.

Final consonants

Khün also uses ᩺ [U+1A7A TAI THAM SIGN RA HAAM​] as a final consonant diacritic.

Note also that ◌ᩘ [U+1A58 TAI THAM SIGN MAI KANG LAI​] has very different shapes in Northern Thai (ᩅᩘ) and Khün (ᩅᩘ).

These characters represent syllable-final consonants, but not all syllable-final consonants are written using these characters.

The superscript consonants in Kühn are limited to final r (᩺ [U+1A7A TAI THAM SIGN RA HAAM​]) and ŋ (◌ᩘ [U+1A58 TAI THAM SIGN MAI KANG LAI​]) in syllables where a subscript vowel prevents the use of a subscript final consonant. Superscript forms are mainly found in handwritten text, whereas regular forms of these consonants in postscript position are the norm for printed texts. o

Subjoined consonants

◌᩠ [U+1A60 TAI THAM SIGN SAKOT​] is an invisible character used to produce a subjoined form of a consonant, eg. ᨠᨠ vs. ᨠ᩠ᨠ. 

Unlike the virama in most brahmi-derived scripts, sakot doesn't kill the vowel between two consonants, nor does it create conjuncts. For example, in ᨨ᩠ᩃᩣ᩠ᨯ cʰ˖ḻā˖d cʰa² laːt⁴ clever the inherent vowel after c is not suppressed.

Also unusually, sakot can follow a vowel-sign. For example, in ᩈᩣ᩠ᨾ sā˖m̱ saːm¹ three the sakot is used to position the final consonant in the syllable below the vowel-sign. This is quite common. A subjoined consonant can also follow a digit, eg. .

Tham will usually attempt to subjoin non-initial consonants, although never more than two 'lines' deep. Sequences of 2 subjoined characters exist, but the second subjoined character joins to the right of the stack, rather than sitting below it, eg. ᨠ᩠ᩅ᩠ᨿᩁ k˖w̱˖ȳr kweːn¹ ox-cart.

A consequence of this shallow subscripting is that a subscript vowel will typically cause a final consonant to not be subjoined, eg. ᩃᩪᨠ ḻūk luːk⁴ offspring. However, this is not always the case. In ᨧ᩠ᨷᩪ c˖bū cuːp³ kiss the final b is subjoined under the onset consonant, and the normally subscript vowel is moved to the side. This arrangement forces the user to enter characters in a different order than they are pronounced.

Subjoined consonants are not only syllable-final consonants. The first consonant in a following syllable may also be subjoined, eg. ᨳᩫ᩠ᨶ᩻ᩁ tʰö˖ṉʻr tʰanon path (final r is pronounced as n). e u654

Not all consonants traditionally have subjoined forms, but modern innovations in borrowed terminology suggest that fonts should provide them for all consonants except the old vocalic letters. u654

Vowels

Consonants carry an inherent a vowel that can be overridden using vowel signs.

Independent vowels

The 6 vowel letters are used in syllable-initial position where there is no consonant onset, eg. ᩑᨠ ẹk eːk³ one. They don't cover all possible vowels in this position: for others it is possible to use ᩋ [U+1A4B TAI THAM LETTER A], eg. ᩋᩧ᩠ᨷ ʔɯ˖b ɯp² starve.

ᩒ [U+1A52 TAI THAM LETTER OO] is not used in Northern Thai. u654

Prescript dependent vowels

Of the vowel-signs, 5 appear to the left of the onset consonant. These characters are typed, however, after the base, as for indic scripts in South Asia. (This differs from most other Tai scripts.)

Other dependent vowels

◌ᩤ [U+1A64 TAI THAM VOWEL SIGN TALL AA​] and  ᩣ [U+1A63 TAI THAM VOWEL SIGN AA​] are both used to represent the same phoneme. The choice of which is used is a matter of spelling. The taller version is typically (Owen says only, for Khün o152) used after ᨷ ᩅ ᨴ ᨵ ᨣ, and avoids confusion with otherwise similar shapes, eg. ᩅᩣ looks like ᨲ. Some textbooks also recommend it's use after ᨧ ᨻ ᩁ ᨽ. e

◌ᩭ [U+1A6D TAI THAM VOWEL SIGN OY​] is not used in Northern Thai. u655

Tones

With the high/low categorisation of consonants, Tham writing needs only the first two combining tone marks above to indicate one of the 6 possible phonetic tones.

The remaining 3 tone marks above are included in the block for Khün, although they are rarely used.

The expected storage position for tone marks is immediately after the base consonant of the syllable, or after a superscript vowel-sign if there is one.

The following chart shows how to tell which tones are associated with a syllable. 'Checked' means ending in the sound -p, -t, or -k.

Diacritics

◌᩠ [U+1A60 TAI THAM SIGN SAKOT​] is used to create a subjoined consonant. See above. 

◌᩺ [U+1A7A TAI THAM SIGN RA HAAM​] is used in different ways in different languages. In Northern Thai it is used to silence one or more characters in a word; in Lü it is used as a final n; in Khün it is used as a final r.

◌᩻ [U+1A7B TAI THAM SIGN MAI SAM​] is used in Northern Thai either to indicate repetition of a word, or to identify double-acting consonants, or to indicate that a subjoined consonant begins a new syllable, eg. compare ᨳᩫ᩠ᨶᩁ tʰö˖ṉrᵃ tʰonra with ᨳᩫ᩠ᨶ᩻ᩁ tʰö˖ṉʻr tʰanon path (final r is pronounced as n). e

◌᩿ [U+1A7F TAI THAM COMBINING CRYPTOGRAMMIC DOT​] is used singly or multiply beneath letters to give each letter a different value according to some hidden agreement between reader and writer. u655

Contextual shaping & positioning

Since there are no conjuncts, there is not so much contextual shaping in Tham as in many other Brahmi-descended scripts.

Placement of tone marks often involves special shaping and positioning. See the positions in the examples below.

In the A Tai Tham KH New font, a second tone mark following ◌ᩢ [U+1A62 TAI THAM VOWEL SIGN MAI SAT​] loses its uptick to create two parallel lines, eg. ᨡᩮᩢ᩶ᩣ kʰeá²ā kʰaw⁵ rice.

Text layout

Text direction

Tai Tham script is written horizontally and left to right.

Text delimiters

Spaces separate phrases. There is no separation of individual words.

The following punctuation marks have "progressive values of finality".

  1. ᪨ [U+1AA8 TAI THAM SIGN KAAN]
  2. ᪩ [U+1AA9 TAI THAM SIGN KAANKUU]
  3. ᪪ [U+1AAA TAI THAM SIGN SATKAAN]
  4. ᪫ [U+1AAB TAI THAM SIGN SATKAANKUU] 

European punctuation like question mark, exclamation mark, parentheses, and quotation marks is also used.

᪣ [U+1AA3 TAI THAM SIGN KEOW], ᪤ [U+1AA4 TAI THAM SIGN HOY], ᪥ [U+1AA5 TAI THAM SIGN DOKMAI], and ᪭ [U+1AAD TAI THAM SIGN CAANG] are all used as section starters, sometimes in conjunction with other punctuation, eg. ᪩᪥᪩ and ᪭ᩣ. e

To close a section, use ᪦ [U+1AA6 TAI THAM SIGN REVERSED ROTATED RANA] and/or ᪬ [U+1AAC TAI THAM SIGN HANG], eg. ᪦᪦᪩, ᪩᪦᪩, ᪩᪦᪩᪬, and ᪦᪦᪬.

Line breaking

Opportunities for line breaking are lexical, but a line break may not be inserted between a base letter and a combining diacritic. u656

There is no insertion of visible hyphens at line boundaries. u656
